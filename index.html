The Ultimate Guide to Gym Muscles: Building Strength and Understanding Anatomy

Written by Amelie Evans, October 27th/ 2024

"If you don't make time for exercise, you'll probably have to make time for illness."

-Robin Sharma


So you want to build your muscles? Look no further. Understanding the key muscle groups targeted in gym workouts and how to train them effectively is crucial in both short and long-term muscle growth. This guide breaks down the major muscles involved in popular exercises, explaining each muscle's function and role in movement. Whether you're looking to enhance your strength, improve your form, or prevent injury, this guide offers insights and exercise recommendations for balanced development across all major muscle groups. Perfect for beginners and seasoned gym-goers alike, it empowers you to maximise results with a deeper understanding of your body's anatomy.


But first, let's look at some of the benefits to cardio and weight training regularly:

  1. Helps you get to and stay at a healthy weight
  2. Reduces your risk of heart diseases
  3. Helps your body manage blood sugar and insulin levels
  4. Can help you manage a chronic health condition
  5. Improves your mental health and mood
  6. Increases your chances of living longer
  7. Improves your sleep

The main muscle groups and exercises

Chest

The chest isn't an incredibly complicated muscle group, with only three muscles making up a large area. The pec major is the one most commonly focused on, as it is the largest of the muscles in your chest. It is the thick, fan shaped muscle above the smaller serratus muscles on your rib cage, and hides the pec minor behind it. The best way to train all 3 muscles is with some of these simple movements:

  • Push-ups (bodyweight exercise)
  • Dips (bodyweight exercise)
  • Incline, wide-grip, partial range-of-motion bench press
  • Bench press (all other variations)
  • Cable flyes (any variation)

Triceps

Some beginners will focus on the biceps for building muscular arms, however the triceps make up two-thirds of your upper arm, so start there! The triceps consist of three different heads: the long head, lateral head, and medial head. The long head runs along the back of the arm and is responsible for shoulder and elbow extension. It is the largest of the three heads and contributes to the bulk of the triceps. Situated underneath the long head, the medial head is less visible but plays a crucial role in elbow extension. Finally, located on the outer side of the arm, the lateral head assists primarily in elbow extension and is often more visible, especially when the arm is flexed. Some good exercises for targeting the triceps are:

  • Narrow grip dips (bodyweight exercise)
  • Cable tricep pushdown
  • Barbell/ cable skullcrushers

Abs

Ab development is one of the most desired fitness goals, however very little people understand the seperate muscles. The abs have four main muscles making up the area. The rectus abdominis is the most recognized, often referred to as the "six-pack," and runs vertically along the front of the abdomen. Flanking it are the external obliques, which are located on the sides and help you twist your body and bend side to side. Beneath them are the internal obliques, which also aid in trunk movement (twisting your body) but work in the opposite direction. The external and internal obliques are generally grouped together as the 'obliques' Finally, the transverse abdominis is the deepest layer, wrapping around the torso to provide core stability and support. So how do you get a 6-pack? Well, ab development is heavily dependent on diet, as you might expect, and simply doing ab exercises will not remove as much stomach fat as you might want. Moreover, the abs are used in a lot of compound movements, such as squats, which is why, despite them being incredibly important muscles for stability, you might never see a powerlifter doing exercises solely focused on the abs. However, if you would like to 'fine tune' what you see, some exercises for building a strong midsection are:

  • Crunches (targets the rectus abdominis)
  • Sit-ups (targets the rectus abdominis)
  • Plank (targets the rectus abdominis)
  • Leg-raises (targets the rectus abdominis)
  • Reverse crunches (targets the rectus abdominis)
  • Side plank (targets the obliques)
  • Russian twists (targets the obliques)
  • Bicycle crunches (targets the obliques)

Biceps

Your biceps brachii have two heads, short and long. They are responsible for elbow flexion, and they act in opposition to the triceps. The brachialis, muscles which sit underneath your biceps, also assists the biceps in flexing the elbow. The brachialis is mostly out of sight and out of mind, but showing it some attention can be effective for rounding out your upper arms and getting stronger. Endless variations of biceps curls exist, but many of them are unnecessarily complicated, or just unnecessary, so try making these exercises your staples:

  • Narrow grip pullups (bodyweight exercise)
  • Dumbell bicep curls
  • Dumbell hammer curls
  • Standing barbell curls
  • Cross body hammer curls (brachialis exercise)

Shoulders

The shoulder joint is one of the most delicate joints in your body and is involved in all upper body movements. The deltoids, located on either side of the collarbone, consist of three main parts: anterior (front), lateral (side), and posterior (rear) heads. Training the anterior deltoids enhances the front of the shoulders, while the rear delts add depth to the back. Focusing on the lateral deltoids makes the shoulders appear wider. Strengthening these shoulder muscles improves overall power and function, and can also create the illusion of a smaller waist, giving a curvy look for women and a "V" shape for men. When you lift weights, it's impossible not to train your shoulders, but if you really want to specifically strengthen your delts, these compound and isolation movements will target them:

  • Standing overhead barbell press
  • Dumbell/ machine shoulder press
  • Dumbell/cable lateral raises

Glutes

The gluteal muscles (or glutes) consist of three main parts: the gluteus maximus, the gluteus medius, and gluteus minimus. The gluteus maximus is the largest and most superficial of the glute muscle, and it plays a key role in hip extension, outward rotation and stabilisation of the pelvis. The gluteus medius is located underneath the maximus and is important for hip stabilisation and movement. It primarily assists with hip abduction (lifting the leg away from the body). The gluteus minimus is the smallest and deepest of the glutes, located beneath the gluteus medius, and also assists with hip abduction and internal rotation. Your glutes is the single largest muscle in your body, as well as one of the most important for athletic performance and looking good, so how do you train it?

  • SQUATS! I cannot emphasise enough that you only need one exercise to train all of the muscles in your legs, and that is squats.
  • Barbell/ machine hip thrusts
  • Dumbell bulgarian split squats
  • Deep goblet squats with a dumbell or kettlebell

Quadriceps

The quadriceps (or quads) consist of four main muscles located in the front of your thighs- hence the name 'quad'. These muscles work together to extend the knee and help with hip flexion, which is why it's very important to have strong quads. The Rectus Femoris is located centrally in the thigh and is the only muscle in your quads that crosses both the hip and knee joints. This dual function allows it to assist in both knee extension and hip flexion. The Vastus Lateralis is positioned on the outer side of the thigh, and is the largest of the quad muscles, so it plays a significant role in stabilising the knee and in knee extension. The Vastus Medialis is found on the inner part of the thigh and is essential for knee extension and the stabilisation of the kneecap. Finally, the Vastus Intermedius is located directly beneath the Rectus Femoris, and is not as visible but contributes to knee extension. Some good exercises for targeting your quads are:

  • Squats
  • Front squat variation (this is hard so not advised for beginners)
  • Leg extension machine
  • Leg press machine

Hamstrings

The hamstrings are made up of three muscles at the back of your thighs. These muscles work together primarily to flex the knee and assist with hip extension, making strong hamstrings essential for movements like walking, running, and squatting. The Biceps Femoris runs along the outer part of the back of the thigh and has two heads: the long head and the short head. The long head helps with both knee flexion and hip extension, while the short head assists primarily with knee flexion. The Semitendinosus is positioned on the inner side of the back of the thigh and assists in knee flexion, hip extension, and internal rotation of the leg. Lastly, the Semimembranosus is located beneath the semitendinosus and also plays a role in knee flexion, hip extension, and internal rotation of the leg.

  • Deadlift
  • Leg curl machine
  • Dumbell Romanian Deadlifts

Back

There are several muscle groups in your back: the trapezius, rhomboids, and lats (or latissimus dorsi).

Your trapezius is a long diamond shaped muscles which extends from your skull all the way down to mid-back, and is responsible for moving your shoulder blades and supporting your arms. A lot of people don't realise that along with your upper traps, which are visible next to your neck, the trapezius also have middle/ lower aspects that shouldn't be neglected.

Your rhomboids are a more neglected group, as they are essentially hidden underneath the trapezius, however they are responsible for retracting and rotating your shoulder blades downward, meaning they are essential for good posture.

Finally, your lats are the biggest muscle in your body, and these flat, triangular muscles run all the way down from your mid-back to lower-back. They and are responsible for vertical pulling movements, and they also help stabilise your upper body during horizontal pushing.

  • Deadlift
  • Lat pulldown
  • Barbell row
  • Low row
  • Wide-grip pull-ups
